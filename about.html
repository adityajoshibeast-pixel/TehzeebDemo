A family recipe book, four generations deep

Our Story

Tehzeeb Dastarkhwan began in 1985 as a single tawa stall outside Aminabad, run by Haji Sharafat Rizvi with a coal handi and his grandmother's Kakori kebab recipe. Within a decade the family opened a proper dining room, then a second, then a third — each one keeping the same dum-pukht process, the same butcher, the same hand-ground spice blend.

Today the chain has five dining rooms across Lucknow, but every kitchen still uses the original slow-cooking method: meat marinated overnight in raw papaya, ghee and a house garam masala, then sealed and cooked on dying coals for hours — never a flame.

"Dastarkhwan means the cloth spread on the floor for a shared meal — food is hospitality first, dish second."

That's why every table at every outlet still gets a complimentary kebab and sharbat while the main order is prepared, exactly as guests were welcomed back in 1985. The restaurant is now run by Sharafat's grandson, Imran Rizvi, who still tastes every batch of Galouti spice mix before it reaches a tawa.

Signature dish

The Galouti Kebab — pounded over 100 times by hand and finished with a whisper of rose water — is still the dish most people come for, and the one dish that hasn't changed a single ingredient since 1985.
